MIZORAM / ASSAM BOUNDARY

 

 

 

 

HISTORICAL BACKGROUND

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

The British annexed Cachar area into British India in 1832

 

 

vOn 6th November 1849 Mizo Chief Ngura of

 

 

Sentlang attacked SEIPUIA, (also known as Kooki

 

 

Raja) Village, which was located at about 9/10

 

 

miles south of Silchar.

 

 

 

 

vRetaliatory expedition, under Captain Lister was

 

 

launched against Ngura village Sentlang in

 

 

January, 1850.

 

v A meeting was arranged on 16-18 December, 1850

 

between Capt. Lister and Suakpuilala, one of the most

 

influential Mizo Chief.

 

 

 

v The British proclaim their claim over territory which the

 

Mizos refused to accept.

 

 

 

v Sa ui tan (Settlement) ceremony was performed between

 

the British and the Mizos (Lushai) and a boundary line was

 

drawn, which, as described by Col. Elles, Assistant Quarter

 

Master General in his book “Military Report on the Chin-

 

Lushai Country” (pp.27) are as follows :

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

v "The Boundary line extended from a few miles above

 

Lakhipur on the Barak to the northern limits of Hill Tiporrah,

 

passing through the southern extremity of Chuttachurra

 

Range, and the Lushai had been warned that any

 

depredation committed on the British side of this line would

 

be seriously punished."

 

 

vThe Lushais respected this settlement (Sa ui tan) amd there was no border trouble for the next 20 years.

 

 

vThe British, on seeing the potential for cultivation of tea in the areas, gradually encroached into Lushai Territory. This was resented by the Lushais who made formal protest to the British in 1865.

 

 

vThis fact was recorded by Mr. Mackenzie as follow:

 

 

 

vVanpuilala’s (one the Lushai Chiefs) messengers expressed great dread of the advance of the tea gardens up the Sonai, which Capt. Steward endeavoured     to     allay     by     showing                         how advantageous to the Hill tribes the vicinity of a garden would be.” (pp. 229 ibid)

 

 

 

 

vThe attitude of Capt. Steward annoyed the Lushais who began to realise the imperialistic design of the British and as a result there was great tension between the British and the Lushais.

 

 

 

vOwing to the ensuing intense and continual tension

 

 

in the border the British drew a line of control in

 

 

1867. A chain of Military outposts was established

 

 

along    the    line    at    Mynadhar,             Monierkhal,

 

 

Nudigram,      Newarband,      Jalnachurra                       and

 

 

chhuttachura. The line was also known as

 

 

Ronphari which means "battle or patrol line."

 

vIn 1870 the including the control.

 

British prohibited all natives, from crossing

 

their subjects, this newline of

 

 

 

 

vIn 1870-71 Mr. Edgar tried to make a new boundary line which was not accepted by the Lushai chiefs.

 

 

 

 

v1871 a series of successful concerted raids were launched by the Lushais One British citizen James Winchester was also killed and his daughter Merry Winchester was taken captive by the Lushais.

 

vThe British took retaliatory measures by sending expeditionary forces into Lushai Hills and had to be contented to limit their actual line of control on the line drawn in 1867.

 

 

vln 1873 the Bengal Eastern Frontier Regulation Was made and under the Regulation the Cachar Inner Line was notified in 1875 following more or less the line drawn in 1867 or the Ronphari.

 

 

 

vThe British subjects were this line without written Officer of Cachar.

 

not allowed to go beyond permission of the District

 

vThis line was established as the boundary between the Lushai Territory and the Cachar District of British India.

 

 

vA map showing the provincial boundary of the Lushai Hills and Cachar was published by Col. H.R. Thullier, Surveyor General of India in 1893.

 

 

vTo this day, the Mizos regard this line as the traditional boundary between the Lushai Hills and Cachar.

 

 

vThe Govt. of Assam also recognised this line as the southern boundary of Cachar District, as indicated below –

 

 

 

 

v"The Southern boundary of the Cachar District was

 

defined and gazetted under the Inner Line Regulation

 

in August, 1875, and is now marked by 46 boundary

 

pillars, which are inspected nearly every year and kept

 

in good order. A pass is granted to persons allowed to

 

cross the Inner Line at a fee of 8 Annas for each pass.”

 

(Para 86 of Assam Government Note on the Lushais,

 

1873 to 1889).

 

 

 

 

vThe extension of British occupation upto the line of

 

1867 or Inner Line of 1875 had driven away many

 

Mizos settlers from their land north of the Inner Line.

 

 

 

vThe Inner Line of 1875 was officially accepted as the

 

southern boundary of Cachar District of British India

 

by the British India Government (Foreign Department)

 

Notification No. 229-P dated 20th August, 1875 read

 

with the appendix to Assam Forest Manual, 1897).

 

 

 

 

vA portion of land measuring about 509 Sq. miIes of

 

 

the Lushai country and beyond the southern boundary

 

 

of Cachar District of British India was declared as

 

 

Reserved Forest by the Assam Government in the

 

 

Assam Gazette Notification No. 5 dated 17. 10. 1878

 

 

(Assam Gazette 19. 1O. 1878) Without any

 

 

consultation or knowledge of the Lushai Chiefs.

 

vTherefore, the creation of the Inner Line Reserved Forest under the

 

 

Indian Forest Act of 1878 by the British Government inside the

 

 

Lushai Country, and beyond the British boundary was an exercise

 

 

of extra-territorial jurisdiction and invalid in the eye of law. It was

 

 

simply an act of force majeure.

 

 

 

vMoreover, the so Called inner Line Reserved Forest under the

 

 

Assam Gazette Notification quoted above was admitted to be

 

 

Mizoram or Lushai Territory in Assam Forest Manual (Vide

 

 

Appendix to Assam Forest Manual 1897, pp. 57).

 

v It may further be mentioned that the southern boundary of Cachar in British India was clearly demarcated and defined and marked with 46 boundary pillars and this line is same as the Inner Line of 1875, excepting this line or boundary there has never been any other boundary demarcation between Mizoram (The Lushai Territory) and Cachar District.

 

 

v As mentioned earlier several border clashes took place and raids were conducted by Lushai chiefs where even one Englishman was killed and his daughter taken captive.

 

 

v The British also had to send expeditionary forces several times to retaliate and rescue the English girl.

 

 

 

vUltimately in equipped and poor Mizo tribals

 

 

could not stand against the might of the British

 

 

Empire and their land was forcibly occupied in

 

 

1890 and formally annexed in 1895 by the British

ANNEXATION OF MZORAM

 

 

 

v Mizoram (then known as the Lushai territory) formally became part of

 

British India on 6. 9. 1895 under Proclamation No. 1698-E, made by the

 

Governor General in Council (Gazette of India 7. 9. 1895) and they

 

made boundary adjustment in 1904 to define the territorial jurisdictions

 

of the two British District Officers of Cachar and Lushai Hills.

 

 

 

v Since the Lushais had been already subdued, the British Government

 

drew the boundaries to suit their administrative convenience. They also

 

intentionally made arrangement for the settlement of outsiders on the

 

flat lands inside the Lushai Territory, as can be seen below –

 

v“Mr. Fuller thinks it desirable to keep (flat lands ) Within the boundaries of Cachar area which will be colonized by settlers from the district, and not by Lushais.”

 

 

 

v(Extract from Assam Chief Commissioner’s letter No. 589 Foreign 162P dated Shillong the 14th April 1903).

 

 

 

vHowever, this line cannot be identified properly except on map as no actual ground demarcation has been made with erection of boundary pillars.

 

 

v The boundary drawn so arbitrarily without the knowledge of the

 

Mizo people and without any ground demarcation or boundary

 

pillars has been creating problems rather than solving them.

 

 

 

v The present so-called boundary line as shown in the Survey of

 

India Map was made in 1904, altered in 1912 and 1930 without

 

consultation and approval of the Mizos.

 

 

 

v It is imaginary line pushed farther and farther south of the 1875

 

Inner Line depriving the Mizos of the gentle slopes and flat lands

 

for the convenience of outside settlers brought by the British.

 

v For the people of Mizoram, the only known and established

 

northern boundary is the Inner Line of 1875, which was

 

drawn before the Imperial British occupied the land of the

 

Mizos and which consided with the declared southern

 

boundary of the Cachar District of Assam. This is also the

 

boundary recognised by and in actual practice both by the

 

people residing in border areas and even by the Assam

 

Government.

 

 

 

v There are three points which have been regarded as the

 

boundary points between Cachar and Mizo Hills in practice.

 

v These are Kukichera on the Dhaleswari river, Duarband on

 

the old Silchar- Aizawl road and Baga Bazar (Banga Bazar)

 

on the present Silchar-Aizawl road (NH-54).

 

 

 

v Government servants on transit were regarded to have

 

passed the boundary between Cachar District and Mizo

 

Hills at these points (Vide Assam Fundamental Rule 106

 

read with Subsidiary Rule, 137). A Mizo Officer who was a

 

Government servant at that time (now retired) clearly

 

mentioned about this).

 

vTherefore, considering all the historical background

 

 

as well as facts and circumstances of the recent

 

 

past that are still related amongst the Mizos, it is

 

 

not possible for the Mizos to accept the imaginary

 

 

lines, which was drawn without their knowledge

 

 

or consultation as per the 1933 Notification.
